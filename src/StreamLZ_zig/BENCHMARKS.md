# Decompress benchmarks

Measured on Intel Ultra 9 285K (Arrow Lake-S), 24 cores, Windows 11.
Zig 0.15.2 ReleaseFast. C# is `dotnet run -c Release` in `StreamLZ.Cli -db`.

Best-of-3 runs reported.

## Baseline (before Phase 7 vectorization)

| fixture | level | size | comp size | Zig MB/s | C# MB/s | ratio (C#/Zig) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| silesia_all.tar | L1  | 212,797,440 | 100,270,195 |  **514** | 6,518 | 12.7× |
| silesia_all.tar | L5  | 212,797,440 |  77,477,582 |  **487** | 5,576 | 11.4× |
| silesia_all.tar | L9  | 212,797,440 |  53,007,034 |  **370** | 1,593 |  4.3× |

### Observations

1. **L1/L5 are copy-bound** — the ~11-14× gap lines up with C#'s SIMD
   `Copy64Bytes` / `Copy64Add` running 4×16-byte vector loads per iteration
   vs my scalar `copy64Bytes` (8×8-byte). Phase 7 vectorization should
   close most of this.

2. **L9/L11 are entropy-bound** — the smaller 3-6× gap suggests the
   Huffman and tANS hot loops are also doing worse than C# (the C# versions
   use 3-stream parallel Huffman + tight unrolled tANS loops) but not as
   dramatically.

3. **L11 is closest to parity at 3-3.5×** — bitstream decode dominates and
   the copy-path differences matter less.

### Caveats

- C# measurements include its own multi-run warmup and are reported by the
  C# CLI's internal timer; Zig measurements are wall-clock from bash. There's
  some noise in the comparison, but 3-14× is far beyond measurement error.
- Both versions decompress the same SLZ1 fixtures generated by `StreamLZ.Cli`.
- Zig `GeneralPurposeAllocator` used for the dst buffer; no pre-touch / large
  page tuning.

## Phase 7 goal

Close the copy-path gap by replacing scalar `copy64Bytes` and the wide-copy
loops with `@Vector(16, u8)` / `@Vector(32, u8)` on the hot paths, matching
the SSE2/AVX2 patterns in `src/StreamLZ/Common/CopyHelpers.cs`. Target:

- L1/L5: within ~2× of C# (≥2500 MB/s on silesia L1)
- L9:    within ~2× of C# (≥800 MB/s on silesia L9)
- L11:   within ~1.5× of C# (≥800 MB/s on silesia L11)
